Why Mentorship Structure Matters

The research is clear. The infrastructure gap is the problem.

Young adults who were at risk for falling off track but had a mentor are 55% more likely to enroll in college and 78% more likely to volunteer regularly than those who did not have a mentor.

[Source: MENTOR — The National Mentoring Partnership, "The Mentoring Effect," 2014]

Students who meet regularly with a mentor show higher persistence rates, stronger sense of belonging, and greater academic self-efficacy — but only when programs include structured matching, regular check-ins, and accountability systems.

[Source: Eby, Allen, Evans, Ng, & DuBois, "Does Mentoring Matter? A Multidisciplinary Meta-Analysis," Journal of Vocational Behavior, 2008]

Most institutions know mentoring works. The problem isn't awareness — it's that coordinators lack the templates, tracking tools, and reporting infrastructure to run a program properly. That's true whether you're standing something up for the first time or trying to fix the one you already have.

That's the gap these kits are built to close.
